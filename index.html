// 01 — FUNDAMENTALS

What is Circuit Switching?

⚡

DEDICATED PATH

A fixed route through the network is reserved exclusively for a communication session. No other traffic can use this path during the connection.

🔒

RESOURCE RESERVATION

Bandwidth, switches, and links are pre-allocated before communication starts. Resources remain reserved even during silence periods.

📞

TELEPHONY ORIGIN

Originally designed for telephone networks (PSTN). The concept of "circuits" comes from physical electrical connections in early exchanges.

⏱️

GUARANTEED QoS

Once the circuit is set up, transmission delay is constant and predictable — ideal for real-time voice and video communication.

🔗

TDM / FDM MULTIPLEXING

Circuits are created using Time-Division Multiplexing (TDM) or Frequency-Division Multiplexing (FDM) over shared physical links.

🚫

CALL BLOCKING

If all circuits on a link are occupied, new calls are blocked. The Erlang B formula calculates this blocking probability.

Three Phases of Circuit Switching

01

CIRCUIT SETUP

A dedicated path is established from source to destination through intermediate switches.

→
02

DATA TRANSFER

Data flows continuously at a guaranteed, constant rate through the reserved path.

→
03

CIRCUIT TEARDOWN

A teardown signal releases all reserved resources, making the path available for future calls.

// 05 — SOLVED QUESTIONS

Theory & Numericals

THEORY QUESTIONS (8)
1. What is Circuit Switching and what are its three fundamental phases?

Circuit Switching is a method of implementing a telecommunications network in which two network nodes establish a dedicated communications channel (circuit) through the network before the nodes may communicate.


The three phases are:

  • Phase 1 - Circuit Establishment (Setup): A dedicated path is created from the sender to the receiver, and all necessary resources are reserved.
  • Phase 2 - Data Transfer: Data is transmitted continuously over the established path with a guaranteed data rate.
  • Phase 3 - Circuit Disconnect (Teardown): The connection is terminated, and the reserved resources are freed.
2. How does Spatial Division Switching differ from Time Division Switching?

Space Division Switching: The paths in the circuit are separated from one another spatially. Physical connections are made to route the signal. It requires a large number of physical links for high capacity.


Time Division Switching: Uses TDM to share a single physical line among multiple connections by dividing the line's capacity into time slots. Digital data is stored in memory and read out in different time slots (TSI) to route calls.

3. What is "Blocking" in a circuit-switched network?

Blocking occurs when a new call attempt is rejected because all available circuits on the required path are currently occupied. In traditional telephony, this results in a busy signal. The Erlang B formula calculates the blocking probability.

4. Why is Circuit Switching considered inefficient for bursty computer data traffic?

Computer data traffic is inherently "bursty" — short periods of high transmission followed by long idle periods. Circuit switching reserves the full bandwidth for the entire connection regardless of whether data is actively being sent, wasting bandwidth during silence periods.

5. Explain the concept and impact of "Setup Delay".

Setup Delay is the time required to establish the dedicated end-to-end circuit before any data can be transmitted. For very short messages, the setup delay can be much longer than the data transmission time, making circuit switching highly inefficient for brief communications.

6. What are the main applications where Circuit Switching is preferred?

Circuit switching is preferred for applications requiring guaranteed QoS, specifically a constant bit rate and zero variable delay: PSTN voice calls, ISDN networks, and continuous live audio/video broadcasts.

7. What is the Erlang B formula used for, and what are its inputs?

The Erlang B formula calculates the probability that an arriving call will be blocked. Inputs: A — Traffic Intensity in Erlangs (λ × h), and N — Number of available circuits. It assumes blocked calls are cleared (lost), not queued.

8. What is a Time Slot Interchange (TSI) in digital circuit switching?

TSI is the core mechanism of digital TDM switching. Data from an incoming time slot is written into a RAM buffer sequentially. The control logic reads data out in a different order, "switching" the signal to the correct destination's time slot on the outgoing stream.
